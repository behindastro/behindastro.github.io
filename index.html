Funding the people
behind the science.

Large astronomy projects succeed not just because of telescopes, but because of the people who coordinate, facilitate, and sustain them — project managers, consortium coordinators, software developers, and science operations specialists. Behind every result, there are also people disseminating knowledge, communicating science, and making astronomy open and equitable.

Across Europe, these roles have no long-term funding home.

behindAstro wants to change that.

The problem

The academic funding landscape invests heavily in research and research infrastructure: instruments, computing, and data systems. What it does not fund are the long-term positions of the people who make large-scale projects function — science project managers, consortium coordinators, software developers, science operations specialists, as well as science communicators, and those who disseminate knowledge and drive equity and inclusion — whose work underpins every European facility, instrument, survey, and consortium. This gap is endemic across almost all European countries and affects virtually every large astronomical project.

The gap

These roles do not fit neatly into existing grant categories. They are not "research", so they cannot be covered by research grants. They are not "infrastructure hardware", so investment schemes do not apply. Yet without them, the research cannot happen. This gap is structural and systemic — it shows up across funding agencies and national contexts wherever large-scale astronomy collaborations depend on sustained coordination and technical expertise.

The consequences

The absence of stable funding has real costs. When positions turn over repeatedly, projects are forced to rebuild institutional knowledge and personal networks from scratch — leading to delays, increased costs, and loss of continuity. The uncertainty also drives talented people out of astronomy altogether: academia is competing with industry for technical experts, managers, communicators, and outreach professionals alike, and precarious contracts are a losing proposition. Precarious conditions also make these careers less accessible, compounding existing barriers to diversity in the field. The toll on the welfare of the people in these roles, and on the projects that depend on them, is significant and growing.
